I competed in FIRST robotics the very second covid ended up until my senior year. I was on FTC team 16391 and FRC team 2383. For those who dont know, FIRST robotics is a highschool robotics competition aimed at creating and competing highly technical and complicated robots. FTC (FIRST tech challenge) is for younger students, the robots are 18x18 inches and played on small fields typically in teams of two. FRC (FIRST robotics competition) is for older students. The robots size restrictions can change every year but typically the maximum weight is 135 lbs.

This robot was my FTC robot in the 2021-2022 season. It served as my first experience with any kind of real engineering and prompted me to delve deeper into the field. From this robot I learned design principles, basic manufacturing, 3d printing for structural purposes, and much more. I owe this robot alot, not only did I get to play a large part in the design and creation of it, but I was its driver. Much of my FRC career and future projects are built on the knowledge I began learning with this robot.

This is the first of three FRC robots I was responsible for building (Charged Up 2024). This one I was least responsible for as I was a junior and this year our mechanical department was oversaturated. However, I took this opportunity to over-design the parts I was responsible for and ended up being solely in charge of manufacturing the robot. I took this chance to learn more for myself and begin teaching my underclassmen so I could rely on them in my senior year. I also served on the drive team for this robot and developed my repair skills in a time-sensitive scenario. This year our team also tried to develop differential swerve, a very unexplored area of FRC drivetrains where two motors run a differential gearbox with an inner gear stack to mimic a coaxial swerev drive train but have a higher torque in a straight line.

The robot seen here is our off-season robot for the same game as the previous season. By this point, I was a senior director of the team in charge of robot design and build. The goal of off-season is to replay the seasons game and outdo ourselves. We started late and had very little time before the first off-season competetion. On top of this, my co-driector and I had never designed a robot alone before. We won both off-season competitions we went to. This prepared me most for my senior season as I not only designed and built this bot, but was its driver and lead pit member. This not only set me up to design more robots (and massivley overhaul our teams design process with the help of my co-director) but understand how a robot's pit works and how to work in team-based, stressful enviroments.

This robot was our teams robot for the 2024 Crescendo season. By this point I was the mechanical director of the robot and the pit captain and technician at each competition. This season we made the most iterations of robots in any season our team has competed in. The first iteration (a massive failure) can be seen in the third image below (a cad). This season the biggest lesson I learned is designing for controllability. I learned more about software on the robot and how we can build better for controllability. This season I also did the most teaching Ive done, running workshops for the underclassmen and delegating work to them to make as many iterations as we did.
